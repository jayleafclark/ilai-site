To add captions to video, you either auto-generate a transcript with software like CapCut, Descript, or Rev, then edit it for accuracy, timing, and style, or you burn in captions manually using an editor's built-in caption tool. Most creators land on a hybrid: auto-generate first, then hand-correct every line before export. Done right, this single step can double watch time, because 85 percent of social video is watched with sound off, according to Meta's own advertiser data.

## How do you add captions to video, step by step?

The process is the same whether you're editing a 30-second clip or a 40-minute long-form video. Only the tooling changes.

1. **Generate a rough transcript.** Tools like Descript, CapCut, Veed, or Rev pull an automatic transcript in minutes. Accuracy on clean audio typically runs 90-95 percent.
2. **Correct the errors by hand.** Names, brand terms, and industry jargon are where auto-transcription fails most. A 60-second clip usually takes 3-5 minutes to proofread properly.
3. **Break lines for readability.** Keep each caption line to 4-6 words on mobile. Longer lines get cut off or force viewers to pause, which kills momentum.
4. **Time the captions to speech, not to the beat.** Captions should appear the moment a word is spoken, not half a second early or late. Drift over 100 milliseconds is noticeable and reads as sloppy.
5. **Style for contrast.** White text with a dark outline or box works across 90 percent of backgrounds. Save color highlights for keywords you want to emphasize.
6. **Export burned-in captions for social.** Platform-native captions (TikTok's auto-caption, Instagram's auto-caption) are convenient but often mistime words and use a font that doesn't match your brand. Burning captions into the video file gives you full control.

## Why captions double watch time

The sound-off stat gets quoted often, but the mechanism matters more than the number. When a viewer scrolls into a video without sound, captions are the only thing keeping them past the first two seconds. No captions means no context, and no context means a swipe.

Captions also help with comprehension speed. Viewers process spoken and written language through different channels, so pairing them reduces the cognitive load of following fast speech or unfamiliar accents. That's part of why platforms like LinkedIn and YouTube Shorts show measurably longer average view duration on captioned clips compared to uncaptioned ones from the same account.

There's a second effect worth naming: captions make video scannable. A viewer can glance at three seconds of text and decide whether the rest is worth their time, the same way a headline sells an article. That's a retention decision happening in under a second, and captions are doing the selling.

## Auto-captions vs manual editing: what actually matters

Auto-caption tools have gotten good. Descript and CapCut both handle clean studio audio at 93-97 percent word accuracy. The gap isn't accuracy anymore, it's judgment.

Software doesn't know that your guest's last name is spelled with two Ls, or that "cortisol" isn't "corn oil." It doesn't know which sentence deserves a pause for emphasis, or which line should break early so a punchline lands clean. That's the work a human editor does after the machine has done the heavy lifting.

## Styling captions so they get watched, not skipped

Style choices sound cosmetic, but they carry real retention weight.

### Font and size
- Use a bold, sans-serif font. Thin fonts disappear against busy backgrounds.
- Keep text large enough to read on a phone screen at arm's length, generally 60-80px for a 1080px-wide export.
- Avoid more than two font weights in one video. Switching fonts mid-caption reads as inconsistent branding.

### Placement
- Center-lower third is standard, but check platform UI overlays first. TikTok and Instagram both place buttons and usernames in the lower third, and captions can get hidden behind them.
- Leave at least 10 percent margin from any screen edge.

### Emphasis
- Highlight one keyword per sentence in a contrasting color, not every noun. Over-highlighting trains viewers to ignore the highlight entirely.
- Match caption animation speed to speech rhythm. A caption that pops in too fast for a slow speaker feels mismatched.

## Common mistakes that hurt retention

- **Captioning music lyrics as if they're dialogue.** It clutters the frame and adds nothing.
- **Using default platform fonts on every video.** This is the fastest way to look like every other account in the feed.
- **Skipping proofreading on names and numbers.** A mistranscribed statistic undermines credibility faster than almost any other error.
- **Ignoring caption files for accessibility.** A burned-in caption helps sighted viewers with sound off, but a proper SRT or VTT file is what screen readers and search engines actually use. Both matter, and they're not the same file.

## FAQ

### Do captions help with SEO?
Yes. A properly formatted transcript or SRT file gives search engines and platform algorithms readable text tied to your video, which can improve discoverability on YouTube and on-site video pages.

### What's the difference between subtitles and captions?
Subtitles assume the viewer can hear the audio and just need translated or transcribed dialogue. Captions include non-speech audio cues like [music] or [applause], which matters for accessibility compliance.

### How long does it take to caption a video properly?
For a well-recorded 60-second clip, expect 5-10 minutes total: 1-2 minutes to auto-generate, 3-5 minutes to correct and time, and a few more to style and export.
